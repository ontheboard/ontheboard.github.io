She Plays To Win

Women in chess are under-represented. Lorin D'Costa in 'She Plays to Win' wrote about this:

'My older sister Emma used to (does still?) take great delight in defeating me at golf, tennis and other games and sports. She even used to play chess, until she declared age ll 'that it could be your thing'. That was very generous of her :-).

Certainly playing for her women's football team, I saw stigma attached to this. Back in the 1990s football was seen as a 'boys thing' and these girls were trying to break this image. Why couldn't they get involved too?

As I write in 2021 we can see huge changes have been made. Women's football amongst other sports, are very popular. In terms of chess, however, it is still unclear.

This one has confused me as I have been a full time chess coach for around 15 years. Chess is not a physical game, and hence I refer you back to the quote by Susan Polgar ['If we talk about pure abilities and skills, I believe there should be no reason why women cannot play as well as men.'] - why on earth should girls not play chess as much as boys?'

See also sheplaystowin.co.uk or the book of the same name.

Without further ado, there follow the short biographies of just a few of the talented women who do play chess.

Jū Wénjūn

Women's World Champion since 2018. Wénjūn plays for the Shanghai Chess Club. In 2017 she achieved a peak rating of 2604.

Phiona Mutesi

The subject of the book and the film Queen of Katwe, Phiona is a Women Candidate Master with a peak rating of 1686.

Zsuzsa Polgár

One of three Hungarian sisters ranked very highly. Zsuzsa (Susan) was Women's World Chess Champion from 1996 to 1999.

Judit Polgár

One of three Hungarian sisters ranked very highly. Judit is widely regarded as the strongest female player of all time, having a peak rating of 2735.

Zsófia Polgár

One of three Hungarian sisters ranked very highly. Now lives in Israel. Zsófia (Sofia) is an International Master with a peak rating of 2450.

Jovanka Houska

Born 10 June 1980

Born in South London of Czech descent.

Peak rating of 2433.

British and English Women's Champion 2008, defending the title from 2009 to 2012, and regaining it in 2016 to defend it in 2017, 2018, and 2019.

Women's winner for the Commonwealth Championship 2001.

Vera Menchik

16 February 1906 - 26 June 1944

Women's World Chess Champion from 1927 to 1944

The first Women's World Chess Champion. She and her sister were tragically killed in London in the penultimate year of the war by a V1 flying bomb.

Vera and Olga were Russian born Czechoslovak chess players with a Czech father and English mother. The family moved to England in 1921 because of the Russian Revolution.

Vera had a positive tournament record against World Champion Max Euwe.

Olga Menchik

2 May 1907 - 26 June 1944

Olga Menchik was a strong chess player and sister to Vera Menchik, the first Women's World Champion.
