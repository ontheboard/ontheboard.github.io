Jū Wénjūn

Women's World Champion since 2018. Wénjūn plays for the Shanghai Chess Club. In 2017 she achieved a peak rating of 2604.

Zsuzsa Polgár

One of three Hungarian sisters ranked very highly. Zsuzsa (Susan) was Women's World Chess Champion from 1996 to 1999.

Judit Polgár

One of three Hungarian sisters ranked very highly. Judit is widely regarded as the strongest female player of all time, having a peak rating of 2735.

Zsófia Polgár

One of three Hungarian sisters ranked very highly. Now lives in Israel. Zsófia (Sofia) is an International Master with a peak rating of 2450.

Jovanka Houska

Born 10 June 1980

Born in South London of Czech descent.

Peak rating of 2433.

British and English Women's Champion 2008, defending the title from 2009 to 2012, and regaining it in 2016 to defend it in 2017, 2018, and 2019.

Women's winner for the Commonwealth Championship 2001.

Vera Menchik

16 February 1906 - 26 June 1944

Women's World Chess Champion from 1927 to 1944

The first Women's World Chess Champion. She and her sister were tragically killed in London in the penultimate year of the war by a V1 flying bomb.

Vera and Olga were Russian born Czechoslovak chess players with a Czech father and English mother. The family moved to England in 1921 because of the Russian Revolution.

Vera had a positive tournament record against World Champion Max Euwe.

Olga Menchik

2 May 1907 - 26 June 1944

Olga Menchik was a strong chess player and sister to Vera Menchik, the first Women's World Champion.

John Leonard Watson

John Watson is an American chess author of a large number of books that communicate ideas very clearly. Many of them are on chess openings. He has been an International Master since 1979.

Mark Dvoretsky

December 9, 1947 - September 26, 2016

Russian chess teacher, author of many well regarded books, amongst them Attack and Defence (1998) and Dvoretsky's Endgame Manual (2003). His books have had reprints from two major chess publishers.

Leonid Stein

1934 - 1973

Ukrainian champion 1960-1962

Stein was the strongest player never to (have been allowed to) participate in a Candidates' Tournament.

Vladimir Vuković

26 August 1898 - 18 November 1975

Croatian author of the classic The Art of Attack in Chess (Oxford-London 1963).

Aron Nimzowitsch

7 November 1886 - 16 March 1935

Amongst the best chess players in the world, he never managed to find funding for a World Championship bid; this was at a time when the challenger had to provide sufficient funds to interest the reigning World Champion enough to put the title on the line. Perhaps he could have been World Champion, but not if he had to play Capablanca, against whom he failed to score victories.

Along with Réti and Tartakower, Nimzowitsch was one of the pioneers of the hypermodern style of play. He wrote three books on chess strategy, two of which are classics of chess literature My System (1925) and Praxis of My System (1929). The third book, The Blockade (1925) goes over ideas already covered in My System. Nimzowitsch's My System is perhaps the most influential chess book of all time, notable for founding or developing principles of blockade, prophylaxis, and overprotection.

Richard Réti

28 May 1889 - 6 June 1929

One of the pioneers of the hypermodern style of play of the early 20th century.

Defeated Capablanca in 1924, breaking an eight year run without a loss for the World Champion. Probably could have become World Champion, but died young (from Scarlet Fever) before he could take the title.

Author of Masters of the Chess Board (1933), which delves into the development of chess and the openings.

Alexander Dmitrievich Petrov

February 12, 1794 - April 22, 1867

Author of the first chess handbook in Russian.

Petrov's Defence (or the Russian Defence) is named after him.

François-André Danican Philidor

7 September 1726 - 31 August 1795

Well known French musical composer, widely regarded as the best chess player of his time.

Author of Analyse du jeu des Échecs (1749), which is notable for its discussion of the handling of pawns, with which Philidor took a huge step forwards from his contemporaries.

'pawns ... are the soul of chess: it is they alone that determine the attack and the defense, and the winning or losing of the game depends entirely on their good or bad arrangement.' (Abbreviated translation from Philidor's Analyse du jeu des Échecs.)

Ruy López

Circa 1530 to circa 1580

A Spanish chess player and priest. His full name was Rodrigo López de Segura.

For a long while the opening, the Ruy López (sometimes now called the Spanish Game), which is named after him, was the opening which was held to give the player with the white pieces the most chance of victory. He contributed to the theory of this opening and the King's Gambit, amongst others.

Ruy López was the author of Libro de la invención liberal y Arte del juego del Axedrez (1561). He was the strongest Spanish player of his time.

Soltis (1947 to present day) has stated, 'he may deserve the title of world champion, but that title did not exist until the late nineteenth century'. H.J.R. Murray (1868-1955) has indicated that López's analysis in the second book of his treatise was a forerunner of the strong pawn play which Philidor would later elucidate in his Analyse du jeu des Échecs.
